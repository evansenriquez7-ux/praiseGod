# Standard 2.MD.C.8 Context & Generation Constraints

## Node Description
2.MD.C.8: Solve word problems involving dollar bills, quarters, dimes, nickels, and pennies, using $ and ¢ symbols appropriately. Example: If you have 2 dimes and 3 pennies, how many cents do you have?

## Generation Constraints
- **Grade Level:** 2nd Grade
- **Vocabulary:** Real-world contexts involving stores, buying, and piggy banks. Names of coins (pennies, nickels, dimes, quarters) must be explicitly used, and values must be assumed known by the student.
- **Constraints:** Max value involved should generally be under $2.00 (200 cents). When crossing $1.00, test proper notation.
- **Distractors:** Very predictable traps: Add face value of coins without value weight (e.g., 2 dimes + 3 pennies = "5 cents"), misidentifying coin values (thinking nickel = 10c).

## Sample Practice Problems

### Problem 1 (Coin Value Addition)
**Stem:** You have 3 dimes and 1 nickel in your pocket. How much money do you have?
**Options:**
- (A) 35¢ (Correct)
- (B) 4¢ (Trap: Counted the number of coins instead of their value)
- (C) 31¢ (Trap: Thought nickel was worth 1 cent)
- (D) 45¢ (Trap: Added extra 10 instead of 5)

### Problem 2 (Word Problem with Change)
**Stem:** Maria wants to buy a sticker that costs 50¢. She has 2 quarters. Does she have enough money?
**Options:**
- (A) Yes, she has exactly 50¢. (Correct)
- (B) No, she only has 25¢. (Trap: Counted only one quarter)
- (C) Yes, she has 100¢. (Trap: Confused quarter with half-dollar)
- (D) No, she needs 48 more cents. (Trap: Subtracted 2 from 50)
